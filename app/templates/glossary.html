These help you understand if a stock is expensive or cheap relative to the company's value

P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)

Shows how much you're paying for each dollar of the company's profit. If a stock has a P/E of 20, you're paying $20 for every $1 the company earns annually.

Low (<15): Potentially undervalued Medium (15-25): Fair value High (>30): Expensive or high growth expected

P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book)

Compares the stock price to the company's "book value" (assets minus liabilities). A P/B under 1 means you could theoretically buy the company for less than its assets are worth.

<1: Trading below asset value 1-3: Typical range >3: Premium (common in tech)

PEG Ratio (Price/Earnings to Growth)

Adjusts the P/E ratio for the company's growth rate. A high P/E might be justified if the company is growing fast. PEG under 1 suggests you're getting growth at a discount.

<1: Growth at a discount 1: Fairly valued for growth >2: Expensive relative to growth

All-Time High (ATH) & % Off ATH

The highest price the stock has ever reached. "% off ATH" shows how far the current price is below that peak. A stock 40% off ATH is trading at 60% of its highest price.

These metrics show how financially stable and profitable a company is

Debt-to-Equity Ratio

Shows how much debt the company has relative to shareholder equity. A ratio of 0.5 means for every $1 shareholders have invested, the company owes $0.50 in debt.

<0.5: Low debt, conservative 0.5-1.5: Moderate debt >1.5: High debt, higher risk

Free Cash Flow (FCF)

The actual cash a company generates after paying all its operating expenses and capital investments. This is the real money available for dividends, buybacks, or growth.

Positive: Company generates cash Negative: Burning cash

ðŸ’¡ FCF is harder to manipulate than earnings, making it a more reliable indicator.

Profit Margin

The percentage of revenue that becomes profit. A 20% profit margin means the company keeps $0.20 from every dollar of sales after all expenses.

ðŸ’¡ Compare margins within the same industryâ€”tech companies typically have higher margins than retailers.

ðŸ“ˆ Technical Indicators

These measure price momentum and volatility

RSI (Relative Strength Index)

Measures how fast and how much a stock's price has changed recently, on a scale of 0-100. It helps identify if a stock might be overbought (due for a pullback) or oversold (due for a bounce).

<30: Oversold (potential buy signal) 30-70: Neutral zone >70: Overbought (potential sell signal)

Beta

Measures how volatile a stock is compared to the overall market. A beta of 1 means it moves with the market. Higher beta = bigger swings, both up and down.

<1: Less volatile than market 1: Moves with market >1.5: More volatile

52-Week Range

The lowest and highest prices the stock has traded at over the past year. Seeing where the current price sits in this range gives context for whether it's near recent highs or lows.
